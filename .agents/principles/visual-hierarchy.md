# Visual Hierarchy & Directing the Eye

**Design follows the user's intent — not the other way around.** Before placing anything on a page, answer: what is the user here to find, and what's most important to them right now? The visual hierarchy should serve that answer. A page that asks the user to figure out where to look has already failed.

**Use color, icons, spacing, and size to direct the eye deliberately.** Every visual cue is a signal pointing somewhere. Decide where you want attention to land first, second, third — and use the cues to build that path. Visual hierarchy is a tool, not a side effect of layout.

**Make less-important information recede so the important things stand out.** Equal visual weight across everything means no hierarchy at all — the user has to read every pixel to find the one thing they came for. Demote secondary information with smaller type, lighter color, tighter density, or position. Restraint on the supporting cast is what lets the lead stand up.

**Too many competing elements is a UX problem, not a visual one.** Stacking colors, icons, badges, callouts, and data points until everything shouts produces a page that's busy and hard to follow. The fix isn't a sharper signal on the priority item — it's removing the noise around it.

**Build structure and predictability for the eye.** Users learn a page in the first few seconds. Consistent placement, consistent rhythm, and consistent visual treatments across surfaces let them stop hunting and start acting. Predictability isn't boring — it's how the eye stops working and the brain starts.

**Knowing user intent is half the work.** You can't direct attention well until you know what the user is trying to do on this specific page. When intent is ambiguous, the hierarchy ends up ambiguous too. Pin down the primary job first, then design the visual order to serve it.
